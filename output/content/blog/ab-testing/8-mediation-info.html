UNPACKING THE BLACK BOX

Experiments show *if* something functions. Understanding **how and why** to improve theories and policies is the core of causal mediation.

The Central Problem: The Broken Link

Our focus is on understanding the influence of a Treatment (T) on an Outcome (Y), mediated by a variable (M), and specifically, challenges in the latter part of this relationship.

Observational links aren't random; causality requires eliminating confounding factors. The **Sequential Ignorability Assumption** needed for this is often untestable and unlikely.
Outcome (Y)

Randomizing T to M sees M-Y as a correlation only, a significant issue for causality. (See red arrow.)

A Hierarchy of Experimental Designs

Instead of stats after the fact, superior designs are key to testing. Robust experimental planning builds stronger causal arguments.

1. Measurement-of-Mediation

Here are a few rewritten options, maintaining a similar size and conveying the core meaning: * **Randomize T; measure M and Y. A common, simple but flawed approach.** * **Start with randomized T; observe M and Y. It's typical, yet limited.** * **T is randomized; M and Y are measured. A basic, less-effective design.** * **Randomize T, assess M and Y. The standard, often weakest, method.**

LIMITATION: **Option 1 (Concise):** * Dependent on untestable ignorability. Significant bias risk. **Option 2 (Slightly more descriptive):** * Vulnerable to untestable sequential ignorability. Bias is a concern. **Option 3 (Emphasis on the problem):** * Unsound ignorability assumption. High potential for bias.

2. Implicit Mediation (Mechanism Experiment)

Develop a treatment, either inhibiting or replicating mechanism (M), and assess its impact on outcome (Y).

STRENGTH: Here are a few options, all similar in length and capturing the key ideas: * **Excellent for disproving theories quickly. Powerful causal inference.** * **Allows quick theory elimination; Offers strong causal insight.** * **Effective at theory refutation without measurement; Causal weight.** * **Quickly rules out explanations. Provides strong causal power.**

3. Experimental-Causal-Chain

Here are a few options, aiming for a similar length and clarity: * Perform two distinct experiments: the first verifying T→M, the second validating M→Y. * Run two experiments: the first to demonstrate the relationship T→M, the second to show M→Y. * Two experiments are required: one to establish the causal link of T→M, and another for M→Y. * Execute two studies: Experiment 1 proves T→M; Experiment 2 proves M→Y.

LIMITATION: Vulnerable to the "product fallacy" if populations differ across experiments.

4. Parallel Encouragement Design (PED)

Here are a few options, keeping the size roughly similar: * **Boost mediator uptake randomly, leveraging this as an instrumental variable.** * **Employ an encouragement strategy, using it randomly as an IV to promote uptake.** * **Randomize encouragement to boost mediator use; treat encouragement as an IV.** * **To increase mediator use, use random encouragement as an instrumental variable.**

STRENGTH: State-of-the-art. Identifies mediation with weaker, more plausible assumptions.

Case Study: Social Pressure & Voter Turnout

A classic "mechanism experiment" explored social pressure's role in Get-Out-The-Vote mailings. Rather than directly measuring pressure, the study used varied treatment designs to manipulate its intensity. See the outcomes by clicking the buttons.

The "Neighbors" mailing, disclosing voting data to peers, caused a large impact, suggesting social pressure was the primary driver.

The Golden Rule of Mediation

Don't just test for mediation... DESIGN for it.

A strong experimental setup offers clearer causal proof than intricate stats on a flawed design; future mediation hinges on careful planning.
